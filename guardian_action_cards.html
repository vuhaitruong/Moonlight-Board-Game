✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 01 / 24
Courage Level
1 / 5 — Low risk
Bear Witness
Acknowledge without trying to fix or explain
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When someone shares a difficult experience, you stay present with them — without immediately problem-solving, minimising, or redirecting to your own story. You hold the space.
Script
"Thank you for trusting me with that. I hear you. I won't pretend I fully understand — but I'm here, and what you're describing matters."
Why It Matters
Often the most healing response is simply being genuinely heard — not advised, not compared, not fixed. Bearing witness is a skill. It is also a gift.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 02 / 24
Courage Level
2 / 5 — Low-medium
Amplify
Repeat and credit someone's idea so it lands
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When you notice someone's contribution being ignored or talked over, you repeat it clearly, attribute it to them by name, and ensure the room engages with it before the conversation moves on.
Script
"I want to come back to what [Name] said a moment ago — that point about [X] is really important and I don't think we've fully explored it yet."
Why It Matters
Amplification protects credit and ensures that quieter voices — or voices the room has learned to talk over — shape the conversation. Attribution is not optional: it is the point.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 03 / 24
Courage Level
2 / 5 — Low-medium
Make Space
Actively invite voices that haven't been heard
─── ✦ ───
The Action
You notice whose voice has not been in the room and actively create an opening for them — without forcing, but with genuine invitation. You also stop talking when you realise you've been dominating.
Script
"We haven't heard from everyone. [Name], I'd love to know your perspective on this — you've worked closely with this area."
Why It Matters
The best decisions come from the widest range of perspectives. Space doesn't open itself — someone has to create it deliberately and with respect.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 04 / 24
Courage Level
3 / 5 — Medium
Call In
A private, caring conversation about harmful behaviour
─── ✦ ───
The Action
Rather than calling someone out publicly, you approach them privately — with curiosity, not accusation. You describe what you observed and its impact, and give them space to reflect and respond.
Script
"Hey — do you have five minutes? I wanted to share something I noticed in the meeting. I don't think you intended harm, but I'd like to talk about the impact."
Why It Matters
Calling in preserves the relationship and gives people the chance to grow without public humiliation. It's often more effective than public correction — and harder, which is why it takes real courage.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 05 / 24
Courage Level
4 / 5 — High
Call Out
Name a bias or harm in the moment, publicly
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When something harmful happens in a group setting, you name it in the moment — clearly, calmly, without attacking the person. You redirect the conversation to the impact rather than the intent.
Script
"I want to pause on that — I don't think that comment landed the way it was intended, and I'd like us to think about its impact before we move on."
Why It Matters
Silence in the face of harm is read as consent by everyone in the room. Public naming — done with care — sets a visible standard and shows those affected that they are not alone.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 06 / 24
Courage Level
1 / 5 — Low risk
Educate Yourself
Research before asking a marginalised person to explain
─── ✦ ───
The Action
Before asking a colleague from a marginalised group to explain their experience or educate you about their community, you spend time researching independently — through books, articles, documentaries, or existing resources.
Script
(Internal check): "Is there a book, article, or resource I could read rather than asking [Name] to spend their time explaining this to me?"
Why It Matters
Asking marginalised people to explain their own oppression is unpaid emotional labour. Resources exist. Using them first shows respect and preserves that person's energy for work that is actually theirs.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 07 / 24
Courage Level
4 / 5 — High
Hold the Door
Sponsor and recommend someone for opportunities
─── ✦ ───
The Action
You actively use your access and influence to open doors for someone who may not have visibility with decision-makers. You name them, champion their work, and stake some of your own reputation on their behalf.
Script
"I want to recommend [Name] for this — I've worked closely with them and their work on [X] has been outstanding. I think they should be in this conversation."
Why It Matters
Mentoring gives advice; sponsorship gives opportunity. Many people in marginalised groups are over-mentored and under-sponsored. Holding the door requires risking your own social capital — and that is what makes it powerful.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 08 / 24
Courage Level
5 / 5 — Very high
Change the System
Challenge the policy, not just the individual incident
─── ✦ ───
The Action
You recognise that an incident is not isolated but structural, and you take action at the policy or process level — raising it in the right forums, proposing concrete changes, and following up until something shifts.
Script
"I've noticed this is a pattern, not a one-off. I'd like to put forward a proposal to change how we [recruit / review / schedule / evaluate] — can we get this on the agenda?"
Why It Matters
Individual acts of allyship are necessary but insufficient. Lasting change requires changing the systems that produce the incidents. This is harder, slower — and the most meaningful thing an ally can do.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 09 / 24
Courage Level
2 / 5 — Low-medium
Interrupt the Interrupt
Stop someone from being talked over mid-sentence
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When you notice someone being interrupted — especially in a pattern that targets certain voices — you step in immediately and redirect the floor back to the person who was speaking.
Script
"Hold on — [Name] was mid-sentence. [Name], please go ahead — I want to hear the rest of your point before we move on."
Why It Matters
Being interrupted repeatedly teaches people to stop speaking. Every time someone reclaims the floor for an interrupted voice, they reset the social norm in the room — one moment at a time.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 10 / 24
Courage Level
2 / 5 — Low-medium
Ask First
Check what support someone actually wants
─── ✦ ───
The Action
Before acting on someone's behalf or assuming what kind of support they need, you ask them directly. You resist the urge to "help" in the way that feels most natural to you — and listen to their answer.
Script
"I heard what happened and I want to support you. Before I do anything, what would actually be most helpful to you right now?"
Why It Matters
Unsolicited "help" can take away someone's agency and centre the ally's needs over the affected person's. Asking first treats people as experts on their own situations — because they are.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 11 / 24
Courage Level
3 / 5 — Medium
Name the Pattern
Surface a recurring dynamic that others have not named
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When you notice that something has been happening repeatedly — not just once — you name it as a pattern rather than an isolated incident, and invite the group to reflect on what is driving it.
Script
"I've been noticing something over the past few weeks — it seems like [X] keeps happening, and I think it's worth us pausing to talk about why that might be."
Why It Matters
Patterns are invisible to those who benefit from them and exhausting to those who don't. Naming the pattern out loud converts a personal experience into a shared systemic question the whole group must engage with.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 12 / 24
Courage Level
2 / 5 — Low-medium
Use Your Privilege
Leverage access others don't have to open a door
─── ✦ ───
The Action
You recognise an advantage you have — access to a person, a room, a network — and actively use it to benefit someone who does not have that access. You name your privilege and put it to work.
Script
"I know [senior leader] well and can make an introduction. Let me set that up for you — I think it's a connection that could be really valuable."
Why It Matters
Privilege is not a problem in itself — it becomes one when it remains unexamined and unused. Deploying access in service of equity is one of the most tangible acts of allyship available to those who hold it.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 13 / 24
Courage Level
3 / 5 — Medium
Correct the Record
Restore accurate credit after it has been misattributed
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When you notice that credit for someone's idea or work has been given to the wrong person — in a meeting, an email, or a report — you step in and correct it clearly and without drama.
Script
"Just to give credit where it's due — that approach was actually [Name]'s idea, first raised in last Thursday's session. I want to make sure that's clear."
Why It Matters
Misattributed credit costs people professionally. Restoring it — even when awkward, even after the moment has passed — is an act of integrity and a signal that accuracy about contribution matters in this team.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 14 / 24
Courage Level
2 / 5 — Low-medium
Share the Mic
Voluntarily step back so others can step forward
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When invited to speak, lead, or present — you consider whether someone with less access or visibility could do this instead. You actively share or redirect opportunities rather than accumulating them.
Script
"Actually, [Name] has much deeper expertise on this than I do — I'd recommend having them present to the leadership team instead. I can support them in preparing."
Why It Matters
Visibility creates opportunity. Those who already have access to platforms tend to accumulate more. Choosing to share rather than add to your own profile is one of the quietest and most powerful acts of allyship.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 15 / 24
Courage Level
4 / 5 — High
Disagree Upward
Challenge bias when it comes from someone with power over you
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When a manager, senior leader, or authority figure says or does something harmful — even unintentionally — you find a way to raise it, even knowing there is a power imbalance and potential personal risk.
Script
"I respect your view and I want to share a different perspective — I think [what was said/done] may have had an impact that wasn't intended. Would you be open to hearing that?"
Why It Matters
The most impactful harm often comes from people in power. Challenging upward is the highest-risk form of allyship — and the one that most directly changes the culture of an organisation from the inside.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 16 / 24
Courage Level
1 / 5 — Low risk
Normalise Difference
Include diverse references naturally in everyday conversation
─── ✦ ───
The Action
In casual conversation, presentations, and examples, you naturally include references that reflect diverse experiences — different cultures, abilities, family structures, and identities — without making a point of it.
Script
(When giving an example): "Say your colleague takes time off for Eid, or a team member needs to leave for school pickup..." — include diversity in your hypotheticals as the norm.
Why It Matters
When diverse experiences appear only in "diversity conversations," they remain exceptional. When they appear in everyday language and examples, they become part of the fabric of how the organisation thinks about people.

Courage Level
3 / 5 — Medium
Refuse the Burden
Push back when DEI labour is assigned to the wrong person
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When you notice that the only person of colour, woman, or disabled person is being asked to lead diversity work on top of their actual job, you raise it — and offer to share that labour or ensure it is properly resourced.
Script
"I want to flag something — I've noticed that [Name] tends to be the default person for DEI questions. That's not equitable. I'd like to share that work. Where can I take something off their plate?"
Why It Matters
Placing the burden of an organisation's inclusion failures on the people most harmed by them is a double injustice. Redistribution of this labour is one of the most concrete acts of allyship an organisation can take.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 18 / 24
Courage Level
2 / 5 — Low-medium
Get the Pronunciation Right
Learn and use someone's actual name
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When you are unsure how to pronounce a colleague's name, you ask them — once, privately, with genuine care. You then practise until you get it right consistently, and correct others when they don't.
Script
"I want to make sure I'm saying your name correctly — could you say it for me? I'll get it right." And later: "It's actually [correct pronunciation] — let me make sure we get that right."
Why It Matters
A name is a person's most fundamental identity. Mispronouncing it repeatedly — especially when it's "too hard" — signals that their difference is an inconvenience. Getting it right signals they are worth the effort.

Courage Level
5 / 5 — Very high
Walk Away from the Table
Decline an opportunity when it comes at someone else's expense
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When offered an opportunity that you recognise was given to you — consciously or not — because of bias rather than merit, you raise it and suggest the process be reconsidered or the opportunity extended to others.
Script
"I'm honoured to be considered, but I noticed [Name] wasn't included in this conversation — and I think they should have been. Can we revisit how this opportunity was scoped?"
Why It Matters
This is the highest act of allyship — giving up something you could have kept. It demonstrates that you understand equity is not abstract. It also demonstrates to organisations that their biased processes have visible costs.

2 / 5 — Low-medium
Check the Guest List
Notice who's missing before the meeting starts
─── ✦ ───
The Action
Before a significant meeting or event, you look at who's been invited and proactively ask whether the right people — especially those from underrepresented groups most affected by the topic — are in the room.
Script
"Before we finalise the invite list, I want to check — are we missing anyone whose perspective is important to this decision? Specifically, who does this affect most?"
Why It Matters
Decisions about people are frequently made without those people in the room. Once the decision is made, the omission is invisible. Preventing it requires someone willing to ask the question before the door closes.

Courage Level
3 / 5 — Medium
Say "I Got It Wrong"
Acknowledge and own a mistake without over-explaining
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When you realise you said or did something harmful — even if it was unintentional — you acknowledge it directly to the person affected, without lengthy justification or making it about your feelings.
Script
"I've been thinking about what I said, and I got it wrong. I'm sorry. What I should have done was [X]. I'll do better."
Why It Matters
The quality of an apology is measured by the absence of "but." A real acknowledgment takes responsibility without redirecting attention to intent. It models the culture that makes accountability feel possible for everyone.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 22 / 24
Courage Level
2 / 5 — Low-medium
Follow Up
Check in after a difficult moment, not just during it
─── ✦ ───
The Action
After a difficult incident, you check in with the person who was affected — not in the immediate aftermath when they may be managing their response, but a day or two later when the moment has passed.
Script
"Hey — I've been thinking about what happened in the meeting on Tuesday. I just wanted to check in and see how you're doing. No pressure to talk about it — just wanted you to know I haven't forgotten."
Why It Matters
People who experience harm often feel forgotten once the moment passes. Following up communicates that what happened mattered beyond the room — and that the person matters beyond their role.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 23 / 24
Courage Level
3 / 5 — Medium
Sit with Discomfort
Stay in the conversation when it gets hard
─── ✦ ───
The Action
When a conversation about bias or discrimination makes you feel defensive, guilty, or overwhelmed, you resist the urge to shut it down, change the subject, or walk away — and instead stay present with the discomfort.
Script
(Internal): "I am feeling defensive right now. That feeling is data about me — not evidence that this conversation is unfair. I will stay in it."
Why It Matters
Growth happens at the edge of comfort. When those with privilege exit difficult conversations, they leave behind the people for whom discomfort is not optional. Staying is an act of solidarity.

✴ GUARDIAN ACTION 24 / 24
Courage Level
4 / 5 — High
Make the Invisible Visible
Surface hidden data or patterns and bring them to leadership
─── ✦ ───
The Action
You gather data about a pattern you've noticed — promotions, speaking time, project assignments, pay — and present it clearly to decision-makers, making the invisible visible in a form that demands a response.
Script
"I've put together some data I'd like to share. Over the last two years, [X pattern] appears in our [promotions / assignments / pay]. I think we need to look at what's driving this."
Why It Matters
Organisations rarely change because of feelings alone — they change when patterns are documented and named in terms they can act on. Data transforms a personal experience into an organisational problem that requires an organisational solution.
