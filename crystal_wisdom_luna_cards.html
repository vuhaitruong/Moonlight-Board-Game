◆ Crystal Wisdom01 / 18
Intersectionality
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The way multiple aspects of a person's social identities — race, gender, class, disability, religion — combine to create unique experiences of discrimination and privilege that cannot be understood by examining one identity alone.
The Research
Dr. Kimberlé Crenshaw coined this term in 1989, showing that Black women faced discrimination distinct from racism against Black men and sexism against white women — their experience sat at the intersection of both, invisible to systems designed for only one.
The Question
When you think about a colleague facing challenges — are you seeing their whole person, or only one part of their identity?

◆ Crystal Wisdom02 / 18
Code-Switching
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The practice of adjusting your language, tone, appearance, and behaviour to fit the dominant culture of a space — often done by people from marginalized groups to be accepted, respected, or simply safe.
The Research
A 2019 Harvard Business Review study found 75% of Black professionals actively code-switch at work. The emotional labour required creates chronic stress that impacts performance, wellbeing, and sense of belonging over time.
The Question
What parts of yourself have you hidden or adjusted to be taken seriously at work? What would it mean to not have to?

◆ Crystal Wisdom03 / 18
Allyship vs. Performative Allyship
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
True allyship involves consistent, behind-the-scenes action that challenges inequity and centres those most affected. Performative allyship prioritizes the ally's visibility over the actual needs of the marginalized group.
The Research
Research shows that performative diversity gestures (rainbow logos, one-day training) can reduce genuine commitment via a "moral licensing" effect — people feel absolved without doing the real, sustained work.
The Question
Think of a time you supported a cause. Was it driven by genuine impact — or by how it made you look? What would deeper action look like?

◆ Crystal Wisdom04 / 18
The Broken Rung
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The first promotion from individual contributor to manager is where gender and racial gaps are largest. When women and people of colour are passed over at this critical step, underrepresentation compounds at every level above.
The Research
McKinsey's Women in the Workplace 2023 found that for every 100 men promoted to manager, only 87 women were promoted — and only 73 women of colour. This single gap is the primary driver of underrepresentation in senior leadership.
The Question
Who in your team is being overlooked for their first step up? What assumptions might be quietly shaping those decisions?

◆ Crystal Wisdom05 / 18
Microaggressions
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
Everyday, subtle, often unintentional communications that send denigrating messages to members of marginalized groups. They are "micro" in each instance but carry significant cumulative psychological weight.
The Research
Research by Dr. Derald Wing Sue shows microaggressions cause more psychological harm than overt discrimination — partly because their subtlety makes them hard to name, prove, or address, leaving targets constantly second-guessing themselves.
The Question
Have you ever dismissed something as "not a big deal" that someone told you hurt them? What gets in the way of simply believing them?

◆ Crystal Wisdom06 / 18
Psychological Safety
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The shared belief that a team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — that you can speak up, make mistakes, ask questions, and disagree without fear of punishment, humiliation, or exclusion.
The Research
Google's Project Aristotle (2016) studied 180 teams and found psychological safety was the single most important factor in team effectiveness — more than talent, skills, or individual brilliance combined.
The Question
In your team, who speaks freely — and who stays quiet? What might explain the difference?

◆ Crystal Wisdom07 / 18
Stereotype Threat
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The psychological experience of being at risk of confirming a negative stereotype about your social group — which itself impairs performance, creating a self-fulfilling cycle of underachievement.
The Research
Steele & Aronson (1995) showed that simply reminding Black students of their race before a test significantly lowered scores — even when all students were equally capable. The threat, not the gap, created the gap.
The Question
Can you recall a moment when you felt you had to "represent" your entire group? How did that pressure change you?

◆ Crystal Wisdom08 / 18
Affinity Bias
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The unconscious tendency to favour people who are like us — who share our background, interests, communication style, or appearance. Often called the "mini-me" bias in hiring and promotion decisions.
The Research
Studies show hiring managers spend 30% more time reviewing CVs from candidates with names similar to their own. This bias explains why homogeneous organisations tend to stay homogeneous even without explicit discrimination.
The Question
Who in your life automatically gets the benefit of the doubt from you? What do they have in common with you?

◆ Crystal Wisdom09 / 18
Proximity Bias
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The tendency for leaders to favour employees they can physically see — in the office, in the same city, visible in meetings — over remote or quieter workers, regardless of actual performance or output.
The Research
Post-pandemic research shows remote workers — disproportionately caregivers, people with disabilities, and those in lower cost-of-living areas — receive fewer promotions and lower performance ratings than in-office peers doing equivalent work.
The Question
How do you ensure your assessment of someone's contribution isn't shaped by how often you physically see them?

◆ Crystal Wisdom10 / 18
The Double Bind
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The impossible situation where women and marginalised leaders face competing expectations — if assertive, they're "aggressive"; if warm, they're "not leadership material." There is no acceptable way to exist under this standard.
The Research
A 2007 Catalyst report found women leaders were penalised whether they adopted stereotypically "male" traits (seen as cold) or "female" traits (seen as weak). Men faced no equivalent penalty for the same behaviours.
The Question
Have you ever judged someone's leadership style differently based on their identity? What was the standard you were actually applying?

◆ Crystal Wisdom11 / 18
Imposter Syndrome
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The persistent internalized belief that you don't deserve your success — that you're a fraud who will soon be "found out" — despite clear objective evidence of your competence and achievements.
The Research
Originally studied in high-achieving women by Clance & Imes (1978), imposter syndrome affects 70% of people — but is significantly more intense for those from marginalised backgrounds, where systemic exclusion has created genuine external barriers that make doubt feel rational.
The Question
What's one thing you've dismissed as "luck" that you actually worked hard for? What would it mean to claim it fully?

◆ Crystal Wisdom12 / 18
Covering
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The act of downplaying a stigmatized identity to avoid drawing attention to it — not hiding who you are entirely, but toning it down to fit in. Examples: not mentioning a partner, hiding an accommodation need, masking a mental health condition.
The Research
Professor Kenji Yoshino found that 61% of employees cover at work — including 45% of straight white men, who cover less-visible stigmatized identities like mental health, socioeconomic background, or religion.
The Question
What do you tone down about yourself at work? What might it free in others if you let it show?

◆ Crystal Wisdom13 / 18
The Glass Cliff
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The phenomenon where women and minorities are more likely to be appointed to leadership positions during crises — set up to lead when failure is most likely, then blamed when things go wrong, despite not creating the conditions they inherited.
The Research
Ryan & Haslam (2005) analyzed FTSE 100 companies and found women were disproportionately appointed to boards of companies experiencing stock decline — placed in charge of problems they didn't create, with less support than their predecessors received.
The Question
When a "diversity hire" fails, what story gets told? And what story should actually be told?

◆ Crystal Wisdom14 / 18
Sponsorship vs. Mentorship
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
A mentor offers advice; a sponsor takes action. Sponsors use their own political capital to advocate — recommending people for opportunities, defending them when they're not in the room, putting their reputation behind someone's advancement.
The Research
Research by Sylvia Ann Hewlett shows women and minorities are over-mentored and under-sponsored. Those with sponsors are 23% more likely to be promoted — but underrepresented groups receive sponsorship at far lower rates than equally-performing white men.
The Question
Is there someone you mentor? Have you ever truly sponsored them — spoken up for them in a room they weren't in?

◆ Crystal Wisdom15 / 18
The Confidence Gap
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
Women systematically underestimate their abilities while men overestimate theirs — leading to fewer applications, less aggressive negotiation, and holding back qualified ideas. This gap is the product of socialization, not capability.
The Research
A Hewlett-Packard internal study found women apply for jobs only when they meet 100% of the criteria; men apply at 60%. The issue is not ability — it is a learned belief that worthiness must be complete before asking for anything.
The Question
What have you not applied for, asked for, or raised your hand for because you felt "not quite ready"? What would you tell a friend in that same spot?

◆ Crystal Wisdom16 / 18
Belonging Uncertainty
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The chronic, exhausting experience of questioning whether you truly belong — whether your presence is wanted, whether you were included by mistake, and whether each small setback confirms you don't fit.
The Research
Gregory Walton's research shows that belonging uncertainty is significantly more intense for underrepresented groups — because ambiguous signals (a cold email, a cancelled meeting) that others interpret neutrally are filtered through the lens of potential exclusion.
The Question
Have you ever felt you needed to "earn" your place in a room that others entered freely? What did that feel like?

◆ Crystal Wisdom17 / 18
Emotional Tax
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
The heightened vigilance and resulting drain experienced by people from marginalized groups as they navigate workplaces not designed with them in mind — constantly managing perceptions, anticipating bias, and remaining "on guard."
The Research
Catalyst research found 58% of employees from underrepresented groups reported feeling "on guard" to protect against bias at work. This vigilance reduces cognitive bandwidth available for actual work by as much as 20%.
The Question
How much of your energy at work goes toward doing the work — and how much goes toward managing how you're perceived while doing it?

◆ Crystal Wisdom18 / 18
Culture Add vs. Culture Fit
─── ✦ ───
What It Is
"Culture fit" hiring selects for similarity to the existing team, systematically excluding diverse candidates. "Culture add" hiring selects for people who bring new perspectives, experiences, and approaches that genuinely strengthen the team.
The Research
"Culture fit" is one of the most commonly cited rejection reasons in hiring — and is highly correlated with race, gender, and class similarity to the hiring manager. It is, in practice, a bias amplifier dressed in neutral language.
The Question
When you say someone "isn't a fit" — fit with what? Fit with whom? And who benefits from the team staying the same?

☽ Luna Facilitator01 / 12
Pause & Check In
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
The energy in the room has shifted — someone looks withdrawn, the conversation has become tense, or a card has clearly landed harder than expected.
What to Say
"Before we continue — I want to pause and check in with the group. How is everyone sitting with what just came up? We can take a breath, a moment, or a break — whatever we need."
Facilitator Note
Don't rush past hard moments. The pause itself communicates that emotions are real and honoured here. You don't need to fix anything — just name that you see it.

☽ Luna Facilitator02 / 12
Shift the Lens
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
The conversation is stuck in one perspective, or dominant voices are crowding out others. Use to invite the group to see the same situation from a different angle.
What to Say
"We've been hearing one perspective on this. I'd like to invite us to zoom out — if you were [a different identity/role], how might this feel? What would be different about your experience?"
Facilitator Note
Name the shift explicitly. People need permission to step outside their own frame — this prompt gives it. Resist the urge to provide the answer yourself.

☽ Luna Facilitator03 / 12
Honour the Silence
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
No one is speaking. The silence might be discomfort, deep thinking, or the weight of something just shared. Do not fill it prematurely.
What to Say
"Let's just sit with this for a moment." [Wait 10–15 seconds] "Silence is also an answer sometimes. Is there anything stirring that wants to be said?"
Facilitator Note
Western work culture treats silence as a problem. In this game it is data. Count to fifteen before speaking again. The most important things are often just below the surface.

☽ Luna Facilitator04 / 12
Bridge to Action
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
The group has had a meaningful reflection but the conversation is ending without landing anywhere — floating in the abstract without connecting to real-world application.
What to Say
"This has been a powerful exchange. Before we move on — what's one thing each of us could actually do differently this week? It doesn't have to be big. Just real."
Facilitator Note
Reflection without action is incomplete. Keep it personal and specific — "the organization should" is not enough. "I will" is the goal.

☽ Luna Facilitator05 / 12
The Compassionate Redirect
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
A player has said something that may unintentionally cause harm — an assumption, a generalisation, or a comment that centres the wrong perspective. Redirect without shaming.
What to Say
"I want to gently hold space for another view — [name], I heard you say [X]. I wonder if we might also consider [Y perspective]. What do others think?"
Facilitator Note
Your job is to protect the container, not punish players. The redirect should feel like an invitation, not a correction. Name what you're doing — don't pretend you're not steering.

☽ Luna Facilitator06 / 12
Name the Dynamic
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
Something is happening that everyone can feel but no one is saying — a power imbalance, dominant voices, visible discomfort, or an unspoken tension in the room.
What to Say
"I want to name something I'm noticing. It seems like [observation — not judgment]. Does that land for others? Is this something we want to talk about?"
Facilitator Note
Naming the dynamic often releases it. You don't have to solve it — surfacing it gives the group agency. Use "I notice," not "you are." Be curious, not declarative.

☽ Luna Facilitator07 / 12
Amplify & Credit
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
An important point was made but overlooked, interrupted, or attributed to someone else — a common experience for women, introverts, and people of colour in group settings.
What to Say
"[Name] said something important I want to make sure we heard: [restate their point]. Can we stay with that for a moment?"
Facilitator Note
This is one of the most powerful and underused facilitation moves. Do it in real time, do it consistently — it teaches the whole group how to amplify. Model what you want to see.

☽ Luna Facilitator08 / 12
Hold the Space
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
Someone is sharing something deeply personal or emotional. Resist every impulse to problem-solve, reassure, redirect, or move the group forward too quickly.
What to Say
"Thank you for trusting us with that. You don't need to explain or justify anything. We're here." [Pause. Let others respond before facilitating further.]
Facilitator Note
The most important skill here is subtraction — remove your facilitation impulse. People who share vulnerability need to feel received, not managed. Stay a beat longer than feels comfortable.

☽ Luna Facilitator09 / 12
The Graceful Exit
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
A player is visibly struggling — overwhelmed, tearful, or needs to step back from a topic. Offer an exit with full dignity, no pressure, no spotlight.
What to Say
"It's completely okay to step back from this card. Passing is always a valid choice here. [Name], would you like to take a breath — or is there something else you need right now?"
Facilitator Note
Never force engagement. The act of offering a genuine exit — and meaning it — is itself a message about the safety of this space. If someone steps out, check on them privately, not publicly.

☽ Luna Facilitator10 / 12
Zoom Out
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
The conversation has become very individual — focused on one story or one person's experience. Use to connect the personal to the systemic without dismissing what was shared.
What to Say
"What [name] just shared is so important — and I'm curious: does this feel like one person's story, or does it point to something bigger? Have others seen or felt something similar?"
Facilitator Note
The move from "this happened to me" to "this is a pattern" is where DEI learning accelerates. Don't make this move too early — let the personal land first. Then widen the lens.

☽ Luna Facilitator11 / 12
The Breathing Room
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
The conversation has been intense for a sustained period. The group needs a moment of levity or release before they can continue going deeper.
What to Say
"We've been sitting in something heavy — and that takes real courage. Let's take sixty seconds. Stretch, breathe, shake it off — whatever you need. Then we'll come back together."
Facilitator Note
Regulate the emotional temperature of the room. Hard conversations require energy; people need recovery time or they shut down. A break is not a failure of the session — it is care for the group.

☽ Luna Facilitator12 / 12
Close the Circle
· · ·
─── ✦ ───
When to Use
At the end of the session, or when a major conversation arc is completing and needs to land with intention before the group parts ways.
What to Say
"We're coming to the end of our time. Before we close — I'd like each person to share one word or one sentence: what are you taking with you from today?"
Facilitator Note
Closing rituals matter. Go around the circle in order — no cross-talk until everyone has spoken. End with gratitude. What happened here was real, and it deserves a proper ending.
